Peanuts

Overview

Peanuts is a mobile app I designed as a one-stop-shop for finance-management. This is a solo project as part of my UX design course at CareerFoundry.

Why I chose this project

With stagnant wages, inflation, and high student loan debt, many young professionals struggle to manage their finances and meet their financial goals. Compared to previous generations, millennials are in worse positions when considering assets in bank accounts, stocks, and loans.

Understanding the market

I performed competitive analysis on two finance-management mobile applications.

Strengths

  • Most features remain free
  • Automatically logs and categorizes users’ expenses, saving time
  • Helpful and simple graphics displaying monthly cash flow and spendings

Weaknesses

  • Syncing issues requiring users to re-sync their accounts
  • Poor and unresponsive customer service
  • Lacks a global network and is primarily only reliable in the US and Canada

Opportunities

  • An app that allows couples to manage shared expenses and goals
  • An app that allows users to make transfers between accounts, payments, and even invest
  • The addition of spending analysis tools to identify goals and encourage healthier habits

Threats

  • Other budgeting apps like Quicken’s Simplifi, Personal Capital, and YNAB
  • Data breaches have made users wary about giving applications access to their financial data

Strengths

  • Honest business model - rather than selling users’ data, YNAB charges their users a fee
  • No ads due to their business model
  • Focused on changing users’ behaviors which may be more effective in achieving users’ goals

Weaknesses

  • Costs money which may deter people looking to save
  • Long and overwhelming initial setup
  • No live customer support

Opportunities

  • An app that allows users to make transfers between accounts, payments, and even invest
  • An app that reports on users’ financial health and suggests ways to improve it
  • An app that provides a credit report

Threats

  • Other free budgeting apps like Mint and Persona Capital
  • Data breaches have made users wary about giving applications access to their financial data

Interview takeaways

  • Users who track their spendings and budget, do so in Excel rather than an app.
    The task is time-consuming and tedious.
  • Financial knowledge spreads through social circles.
    Those who lacks finance-savvy friends and family do not know what they “should be doing”.
  • Users have a strong interest in learning how to invest.
    Users of different financial statuses all expressed interest, but don’t think this information is readily accessible.

The bottom line

From the survey results and initial interviews, I narrowed down users’ needs and interests to the following:

Budgeting
Saving
Investing

Since a large population uses payment apps, I wanted to leverage this and create a one-stop-shop app that consolidates a mobile wallet with finance-management functionality and finance learning.

Building the skeleton

Upon completing market and user research, I began to build a foundation for the app by developing user personas, task analysis, user flows, and a site map.

My target audience

From my interviews, I developed three user personas to design Peanuts around.

Brianna, the Fresh Grad

She is focused on creating a budget and sticking to it.

“It would be nice if I could be in a place where I am financially independent and could live on my own comfortably. I can’t afford to do that.”

Ashley, the Saver

She is working towards achieving a large savings goal.

“It’s been easy saving money when it was just my husband and me. We are expected a baby soon, so we have to be more mindful about contributing to our savings and goals.”

Tom, the Investor

He would like to learn more about how to invest.

“I don’t really know where to put money with the stock market crashing. My long-term investment is fine. As for short-term investments and getting a return off that, I don’t know.

Features to include

  1. Account consolidation
    To reduce the time spent checking multiple bank statements and apps.
  2. Mobile wallet
    To provide a commonly used functionality and gain traction amongst other apps.
  3. Budget and savings management
    To help people set and achieve their financial goals.
  4. Forum
    To develop a community where users can share finance knowledge.
  5. Investment tracker
    To view investment activity overtime.

Testing my prototype

Once I had the core tasks prototyped in Figma, I was ready to conduct usability testing.

What am I trying to determine?

  • Does the app provide value to its users?
    Measured by a 7-point scale on how likely users feel they would use the app.
  • Can users complete core tasks easily?
    Measured by documenting error occurrences.

Style Guide

To ensure consistency throughout my app, I developed a style guide outlining the typography requirements, color palette, common UI elements specifications (buttons, input fields, icons, etc.), and general dos and don’ts.

Designing for Accessibility

When designing a product, I aim to make it accessible to as many users as possible. After implementing visual design elements, I reviewed the prototype with a close eye, to ensure it followed accessibility guidelines. I made changes to my design, including increasing the font sizes and using higher contrasting colors.

In the example below, I chose a darker green to meet the recommended contrast ratio of 4.5:1.

Retrospective

  • The project scope was ambitious and large.
  • The project required a solid foundation in financial knowledge, which was obtained through additional research.
  • For future projects, I would consider keeping the scope smaller, so I could narrow my focus and expand on select features.
  • I made changes in the language used in the app to improve usability, but the design could further benefit from a UX writer.
